Where Profit Takes Root

Presentation Overview

  • Predict the profitability of future CravenSpeed products
  • Use existing product and sales data to train a machine learning model
  • Identify key factors that influence product success
  • Keep the model interpretable and only use 10 features
  • Evaluate predictions using Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)

Data Overview

  • Dataset: 2,439 products developed by CravenSpeed
  • Target Variable:
    Profit = Revenue (2019–present) – (BOM Cost × Units Sold)
  • Original Features:
    • Sales channel percentages (e.g., pct_Direct Sales, src_Amazon FBM)
    • Product attributes (e.g., Unit Weight, Number of Components, Product Type)
    • Timeline-based fields like First Order (from sales data) and monthly sales bins

Feature Engineering Strategy

  • Focused on product-level characteristics likely to influence profit
  • Prioritized features that captured:
    • Profitability ratios (e.g., profit margin, BOM efficiency)
    • Sales performance (e.g., log units sold, monthly revenue)
    • Product complexity (e.g., weight-to-component ratios)
    • Use logs for profit modeling
  • Avoid engineering high-cardinality or redundant features
  • Perform transformations on numeric and date-based variables

Final 10 Features

  1. Profit Margin (%) – Profit as a percent of total revenue
  2. Log Units Sold – Log-transformed count of units sold
  3. Profit per Unit – Total profit divided by units sold
  4. Average Monthly Revenue – Revenue normalized by product age
  5. High Margin Flag – Binary: 1 if margin > $50
  6. Component Density – Weight per component (unit_weight_pounds / number_of_components)
  7. Revenue-to-Weight Ratio – Revenue per pound of product
  8. BOM as % of Price – BOM cost relative to retail price
  9. Product Age – Years since first recorded sale
  10. Margin ($) – Retail price minus BOM cost

  • Also has some weird outliers that it predicts

Introducing: XGBoost (xgbTree)

  • What is XGBoost?
    • XGBoost stands for Extreme Gradient Boosting
    • It’s an ensemble method that builds many decision trees in sequence order, using a gradient descent type approach
    • Strong performance with numeric and engineered features
  • Stabilizes variance and improves error distribution
  • Each new tree is trained to correct the errors of the previous trees
  • The model combines these “weak learners” into a powerful prediction engine

Why XGBoost Works Well Here:

  • Profit is nonlinear — small changes in price or units sold can create large changes in profit
  • Feature interactions matter — e.g., margin only matters when units sold is high
  • Handles skewed targets — profit has large outliers and a long right tail
  • Automatically learns thresholds (e.g., what qualifies as “high margin”)
  • Built-in regularization avoids overfitting on rare product types or extreme cases
  • Works well with the model’s mix of engineered numeric and binary features

Model Performance

  • Evaluation Metric: Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)
  • RMSE calculated on log-transformed profit

Final Results:

  • Value is under 0.20, so I am pretty confident in its accuracy in predicting future profit

Insights & Takeaways

  • Profit prediction is feasible and highly accurate with the right engineered features
  • The most influential features were: Profit per unit, average monthly revenue, and profit margin %
  • Feature engineering captured nonlinear effects and normalized skewed values
  • XGBoost used these features to make a relatively accurate prediction model
  • Final model predicts profit with a root mean square error of about 0.09, even across diverse product types
